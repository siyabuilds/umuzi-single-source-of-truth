# BA 101 Facilitators Guide

## What is BA 101?
Business Analysis 101 (BA 101) is a crash course meant to upskill beginner Business Analysts on crucial concepts, deliverables, and skills. The course is split up into four modules:

### Understanding Stakeholders
This module focuses on deliverables that engender project proposition documentation and stakeholder management. The brief utilizes project scenarios to cohesively tie all the deliverables together.

### Strategy Development
This module focuses on strategy development, methods, and deliverables for strategic analysis. The brief utilizes project scenarios to cohesively tie all the deliverables together with the help of extra reading material.

### Data Analytics
This module is an amalgam of beginner data analytics courses that focuses on explaining the importance of data-driven decisions, the role of statistics in data analysis, SQL for business, and understanding data visualization.

### Data Visualization or Reporting
Finally, this module simulates a Dashboard development project in a Tableau course where the cohort can put into practice all the skills they acquired in the previous course.

Each course should be facilitated over one sprint for the duration of 1 week or 5 working days. This works really well with the 5-task structure of the brief, which means 1 task per day of the sprint.

The first day of the sprint will start off with a kick-off presentation where you will have to introduce the cohort to the various components of the course. The components are:

- **The Open Info Document**: This document is a short lecture document that focuses on explaining the importance of the contents of the module and how the content relates to business analysis.
- **The Brief**: The brief contains the module content which comprises the project scenarios, reading material, submission guidelines, and deadlines.
- **The Assessment Rubric**: This is a guideline on how the module tasks will be assessed.

## How do I best facilitate or deliver the course?
We answer this question in two parts: using the course structure to your advantage and curating a customized experience for every cohort.

### Part 1: Curating a Customized Experience for Every Cohort
Let's start with a refresher on your facilitation skills. We attached a great article that focuses on guiding your understanding of how to curate the best learning environment and create opportunities for your cohort to engage with the course content constructively.

You will have to be intentional in how you introduce new topics to the cohort, which requires a set of skills to manage and engage people as a facilitator. Through observations, we have identified that cohorts choose BA 101 for various career needs and interests.

Understanding these needs and interests will be your first step to successfully delivering this course. The delivery will need to be customized to the experience and background of the cohort. This simply means you will have to spend time with the cohort in the first briefing of the course to interview a few of them in one sitting.

The point of the interview is to ask questions like:

1. Why did you choose the Business Analysis course?
2. What is your understanding of the role of a business analyst?
3. Do you have any experience working with a business analyst or as a business analyst?

To save time, make sure you ask the whole team to vote if they share the same views as the peers who answered the questions.

We have attached a great article that will take you through the importance of understanding your cohort's needs and interests.

### Part 2: Using the Course Structure to Your Advantage
The course follows the following structure:

#### Kick-off Session
This will be your first contact with the cohort. Use this opportunity to introduce yourself and your background to engender trust and personalize the experience. Spend some time interviewing some of the cohorts and asking the questions recommended above.

For this one, you will have to read the room, so it will be a good idea to tell the participants to turn on their cameras so you can spot levels of attention and comfort or tension. If necessary, prepare an icebreaker. Spend approximately 15 minutes on this.

Once you have completed the above, take them through the DPD BA 101 Presentation.

#### Open Information Session
The presentation of this session should be like an industry. Be intentional about using the information you gathered from the interviews to create examples the cohort will relate to. Ask open-ended questions about new complex concepts to spark engagement.

#### The Briefing
You will introduce the project scenario or module context in this session. Be intentional about building the cohort's excitement and confidence when approaching briefs and new tasks or deliverables.

Before you start talking about the task, ask questions about a scenario to find out if the cohort is familiar with it. Their answers will guide your decision on whether to further explain it with examples they can relate to or move on. When you finally take them through the task of the day, you want to clearly explain the subject and object of the task. Reiterate and rephrase certain complex concepts if necessary.

#### The Rubric
Use this opportunity to objectively explain what the success criteria are for completing the module tasks. This will also be an opportunity to curate an atmosphere of optimism and confidence. Engage the learners to make sure they understand the depth of the requirements in the rubric.

#### Stand-ups or Review Session
Though this session is meant for the facilitator to follow up on students' progress, it can also be an opportunity to spot similar struggles in the team. The similar struggles will be an indication of either a task subject or objective that the cohort does not clearly understand. Once you identify this, spend some time to clarify it again because of self-contradiction. This means your explanation needs to be consistent throughout the sprint.

#### The Sprint Retro
Retrospectives are a great opportunity to get feedback from the cohort on their experience of the sprint. It is very important that you intentionally create a free speech environment; however, remember to guide the conversation with a structure so it is constructive and not destructive.

Give verbal feedback on all criticism and take responsibility for any downfalls. This is also an opportunity to correct any misguided assumptions from the cohort. Make it clear what you can take immediate action on and what will require a long process to change. This will allow you to control expectations and further build trust.
